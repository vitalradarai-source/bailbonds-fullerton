Where Do Fullerton Arrests Go?

When Fullerton Police Department officers make an arrest, the person is transported to the Fullerton Police Department Jail at 237 W Commonwealth Avenue, Fullerton, CA 92832 (jail phone: 714-738-6800). This city-operated facility is a 24-hour operation, handling the initial booking for virtually all arrests made within Fullerton city limits — including those originating on or near the CSUF campus, at downtown Fullerton bars, and on major corridors like Harbor Boulevard and Commonwealth Avenue.

For serious felony charges — or when the Fullerton city jail is at capacity — defendants are transferred to the Orange County Intake and Release Center (IRC) at 550 N Flower Street, Santa Ana, CA 92703. The IRC is the primary intake point for the Orange County Sheriff's Department (OCSD) jail system, which also includes the Theo Lacy Facility in Orange and the James A. Musick Facility in Irvine. Angels Bail Bonds posts bonds at every one of these facilities.

Once transferred into OCSD custody, the defendant's booking information appears in the Orange County inmate locator — though that database typically updates 2 to 6 hours after the actual booking. We have direct contacts and can often confirm location and bail amount before the public record reflects it. Do not wait for the online database to update before you call us.

Why Fullerton Sees So Many Arrests

Fullerton is the largest city in northern Orange County, with a population of approximately 145,000 — but it punches above its weight when it comes to arrest volume. Several factors converge to make Fullerton one of the busiest booking jurisdictions in the county:

  • Downtown Fullerton nightlife: The area around Wilshire Avenue and Harbor Boulevard hosts dozens of bars, clubs, and restaurants within a walkable radius. Weekend nights regularly produce DUI stops, public intoxication detentions, and fight-related battery arrests.
  • CSUF student population: California State University, Fullerton has over 40,000 enrolled students — one of the largest campuses in the CSU system. Off-campus parties, Greek Row events, and post-game gatherings contribute to alcohol-related arrests particularly on Thursday through Saturday nights and during football season.
  • Major arterials: Harbor Boulevard, State College Boulevard, and the 57 Freeway corridor see consistent DUI enforcement. The California Office of Traffic Safety (OTS) has cited Fullerton for above-average alcohol-involved collision rates among similar-sized California cities.
  • Transit hub activity: Fullerton's Metrolink and Amtrak station at 120 E Santa Fe Ave generates its own foot traffic, and officers patrol the area surrounding the station actively.

The most common charges that result in a call to a Fullerton jail bondsman include:

  • DUI — Vehicle Code 23152: First-offense bail is set at $15,000 on the Orange County bail schedule. DUI with injury (VC 23153) jumps to $100,000.
  • Public Intoxication — Penal Code 647(f): Misdemeanor charge. Bail is typically set low or the defendant may be released on their own recognizance after sobering up — but a hold for other charges or active warrants can change that outcome significantly.
  • Drug Possession — Health & Safety Code 11350 / 11377: Felony simple possession carries $10,000 to $25,000 in bail depending on the substance and circumstances. Possession for sale (HS 11351 / 11378) starts at $30,000.
  • Domestic Violence — Penal Code 273.5 / 243(e)(1): Corporal injury on a spouse (PC 273.5) is set at $50,000 on the Orange County schedule. Battery on a cohabitant (PC 243(e)(1)) is typically $10,000. A criminal protective order under PC 136.2 will likely be issued at arraignment — but that is a court order, not a bail hold.
  • Criminal Threats — Penal Code 422: Bail starts at $50,000. Often accompanies other charges from a domestic dispute or altercation.
  • Stalking — Penal Code 646.9: Bail is typically set at $150,000 for felony stalking under the Orange County schedule.
  • Vandalism / Disorderly Conduct / Trespassing: Common during late-night incidents downtown or on campus. Bail amounts vary but most are in the $500–$5,000 range for misdemeanor-level charges.

The Booking Process at Fullerton PD Jail — Step by Step

The booking process at the Fullerton Police Department Jail at 237 W Commonwealth Ave follows California law. Here is what happens from the moment of arrest to the point where bail can be posted:

Arrival
Transport and Property Inventory
Officers transport the defendant to 237 W Commonwealth Ave. Personal belongings — phone, wallet, keys, jewelry — are inventoried, bagged, and logged. The defendant receives a receipt for their property. All property is held at the jail until release.
Identity
ID Check and Warrant Search
Officers confirm the defendant's identity and run their name through California state and federal databases. Any outstanding warrants from other jurisdictions are flagged here. An active warrant from another county can place a hold that prevents release even after the Fullerton bail is posted locally.
Biometrics
Fingerprints and Photo
Electronic fingerprints (Live Scan) are submitted to the California Department of Justice and the FBI. A booking photo is taken. Criminal history confirmed through this process can affect the bail amount or whether a judge allows bail at arraignment under California Constitution Article I, Section 12.
Medical
Health Screening
California law requires a health and mental health screening for every booked individual. Defendants with immediate medical needs may be transported to a hospital — typically UCI Medical Center or Anaheim Regional — before booking is completed.
Bail Set
Bail Amount Assigned
Bail is set according to the Orange County bail schedule for the charges filed. This is the amount a bondsman works from. Bail can be posted as soon as this step is complete — California imposes no mandatory waiting period after booking is finished.

Misdemeanor bookings at Fullerton PD Jail typically take 2 to 6 hours from arrival. Felony bookings can run 8 to 24 hours, particularly if a transfer to the Orange County IRC is required. The Fullerton city jail is a 24-hour facility — a bail bond can be posted and release processed at any hour of the day or night.

Under California Penal Code § 825, arraignment must occur within 48 hours of arrest (excluding Sundays and court holidays). Posting a bail bond before arraignment locks in the bail schedule amount — waiting until the judge sees the case risks a higher bail amount or, in serious cases, a bail denial.

Domestic Violence Charges in Fullerton — What Families Need to Know

Domestic violence arrests are among the most emotionally difficult situations Angels Bail Bonds handles. Families are often confused about what the charges mean, whether a hold exists, and when the arrested person can come home. Here are the facts as they apply to Fullerton and Orange County:

PC 273.5 — Corporal Injury to a Spouse or Cohabitant

This is the most frequently charged felony DV offense in California. It covers any willful infliction of corporal injury resulting in a traumatic condition on a spouse, cohabitant, or the parent of the defendant's child. Under the Orange County bail schedule, bail for PC 273.5 is set at $50,000 — meaning the bond fee is $5,000 at the 10% rate.

PC 243(e)(1) — Battery on a Spouse or Cohabitant

This misdemeanor DV charge applies when battery occurs without visible injury. Bail is set at $10,000 on the Orange County schedule. Battery in a DV context is taken seriously by Fullerton PD even without visible injury because state law requires officers to make an arrest when there is probable cause in a DV call.

PC 136.2 — Criminal Protective Orders

Many families believe a protective order prevents the arrested person from being released on bail. That is incorrect. PC 136.2 is a criminal protective order issued by the court — it is not a bail hold. A protective order restricts contact with the alleged victim, but it does not prevent a bail bond from being posted. The defendant can be released from the Fullerton PD Jail on a bail bond even with a 136.2 order in place — they simply must comply with its terms or face re-arrest.

The "72-Hour Hold" Myth

California law does not impose a mandatory 72-hour hold after a domestic violence arrest. This is one of the most persistent and damaging myths in California bail bond situations. There is no mandatory overnight hold in California law for DV arrests. Bail can be posted immediately once the booking process is complete and the Orange County bail schedule amount is assigned. Do not wait based on this false belief — call Angels Bail Bonds as soon as you learn of the arrest.
